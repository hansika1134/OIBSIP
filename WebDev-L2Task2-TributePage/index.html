Ada Lovelace

The world's first computer programmer, a century before the computer existed.

Her Story

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was born in London in 1815, the only legitimate child of the poet Lord Byron. Her mother, wary of the "madness" she associated with poetry, steered young Ada firmly toward mathematics and logic — a decision that would shape the course of computing history.

In 1833, Ada met the mathematician Charles Babbage and became fascinated by his designs for the Analytical Engine, a mechanical general-purpose computer that was never built in his lifetime. When Ada translated an Italian paper on the Engine, she added her own extensive notes — nearly three times longer than the original article.

Within those notes was something remarkable: a method for the Engine to compute Bernoulli numbers. Because it was expressed as a sequence of operations the machine could follow, historians consider it the first published algorithm intended to be carried out by a machine — making Ada, in effect, the first computer programmer.

Ada also foresaw something Babbage himself never fully grasped: that such a machine could go beyond mere number-crunching to manipulate symbols, music, and images. She died in 1852 at just 36, but her insight anticipated the general-purpose computer by more than a hundred years.

Key Milestones

1815

Born in London to Lord Byron and Annabella Milbanke.

1833

Meets Charles Babbage and first sees a model of his Difference Engine.

1843

Publishes her translated notes on the Analytical Engine, including the first machine algorithm.

1852

Passes away at 36; her contributions remain largely unrecognized for over a century.

1980

The U.S. Department of Defense names its programming language "Ada" in her honor.

"That brain of mine is something more than merely mortal; as time will show." — Ada Lovelace, in a letter to her mother, 1841
